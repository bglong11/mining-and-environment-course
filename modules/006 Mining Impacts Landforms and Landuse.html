Reshaping the Earth: Land Use & Landform Changes in Mining

Understanding Mining's Footprint on the Landscape

1. Introduction: Mining's Transformation of the Land

Mining, by its very nature, involves altering the Earth's surface and subsurface to extract mineral resources. These activities inevitably lead to changes in land use (how the land is utilized by humans and nature) and landform (the physical shape and features of the land). This lesson will explore the types of landform changes, the scale of land use alteration, and the implications for the environment and future land rehabilitation.

Lesson Objectives:

  • Identify the main ways mining activities alter landforms (e.g., open pits, waste dumps).
  • Understand how mining changes pre-existing land uses and its societal implications.
  • Recognize the significant land area often required for waste rock and tailings storage.
  • Discuss the concept of "residual footprint" after mine closure.
  • Explore principles of landform design for rehabilitation and post-mining land use.

2. Altering the Earth's Contours: Mining-Induced Landform Changes

Mining operations create new landforms and significantly modify existing ones. The scale and nature of these changes depend on the type of mining, the geology of the ore deposit, and the volume of material moved. (Referenced in Chapters 8.2.2, 16.1, 24, & 26 of "Mining and the Environment").

2.1 Excavations: Open Pits and Quarries

Open-pit mines involve excavating large depressions to access ore bodies near the surface. These can become vast, deep voids, dramatically altering the pre-mining topography.

  • Scale: Some open pits can be kilometers wide and hundreds of meters deep (e.g., Bingham Canyon Mine, Grasberg Mine).
  • Features: Characterized by benches (terraces) on the pit walls for stability and access.
  • Post-Closure: Often fill with water to become pit lakes, creating new aquatic environments with unique water quality challenges. (Chapter 26.5).

2.2 Elevated Landforms: Waste Rock Dumps and Tailings Storage Facilities

Waste Rock Dumps/Storages: Large engineered piles of non-ore material (overburden and rock not containing economic minerals) removed to access the ore. These can be among the largest man-made structures. (Chapter 24).

  • Location: Ideally placed to minimize haulage distances and environmental impact, but often occupy significant land areas.
  • Design: Engineered for long-term physical and geochemical stability. Designs include valley fills, ridge dumps, and terraced structures. New fill slopes are created.
  • Environmental Concerns: Potential for erosion, slope instability, and ARD if sulphide-bearing. (Chapter 24.2).

Tailings Storage Facilities (TSFs) / Impoundments: Engineered structures designed to contain fine-grained waste (tailings) from mineral processing, typically mixed with water as a slurry. (Chapter 23).

  • Scale: Can cover vast areas, often requiring large dams to impound the tailings.
  • Impact on Landform: Creates new, often flat or gently sloping surfaces (tailings beaches) and elevated dam structures.
  • Environmental Concerns: Risk of dam failure, seepage of contaminated water, wind erosion of dried tailings.

2.3 Other Landform Alterations

  • Infrastructure Footprints: Processing plants, roads, railways, pipelines, power lines, and accommodation facilities all occupy land and can alter local topography and drainage.
  • Subsidence: Lowering of the ground surface, primarily associated with underground mining due to the collapse of voids. Can create troughs or sinkholes. (Chapter 16.1.3).

3. Shifting Landscapes: Mining and Land Use Change

Mining operations lead to significant changes in how land is used, both directly within the mine lease and indirectly in surrounding areas. (Referenced in Chapters 8.2.5, 10, & 16).

  • Direct Conversion: Pre-mining land uses (e.g., forestry, agriculture, natural habitat, traditional indigenous uses) are directly replaced by mining infrastructure and activities.
  • Loss of Access: Traditional users may lose access to resources like hunting grounds, water sources, or culturally significant sites.
  • Indirect Changes (Induced Development):
    • New settlements or expansion of existing ones to accommodate workforce and support services.
    • Development of new agricultural areas or other economic activities spurred by the mine's presence or improved access (e.g., new roads).
  • Post-Mining Land Use: A critical aspect of mine closure planning is determining the future land use for rehabilitated areas. This could be a return to a semblance of the original ecosystem, agriculture, forestry, recreation, or other industrial uses. (Chapter 26.4.1).

The "Residual Footprint"

Even with best-practice rehabilitation, some landform changes are essentially permanent (e.g., large open pits that become pit lakes, very large waste rock dumps). This remaining altered area is sometimes referred to as the "residual footprint." The goal of mine closure is to make this footprint safe, stable, and, where possible, integrated into a productive or ecologically functional post-mining landscape.

4. Assessing and Mitigating Landform & Land Use Impacts

Addressing these extensive changes requires careful assessment during the ESIA phase and proactive management and rehabilitation throughout the mine life and into closure.

4.1 Assessment:

  • Baseline Studies: Documenting pre-mining topography, landforms, land capability, existing land uses, and ecological/cultural values. (Chapter 9).
  • Visual Impact Assessment: Evaluating how the mine will alter the visual landscape from key viewpoints.
  • Land Use Planning Integration: Ensuring mine development is consistent with, or appropriately amends, regional and local land use plans.
  • Stakeholder Consultation: Engaging with communities and other stakeholders regarding potential impacts on their land use and values.

4.2 Mitigation & Rehabilitation (Chapter 24.3, 26):

  • Minimizing Footprint: Optimizing mine layout and design to reduce the area of disturbance. For example, using in-pit disposal of waste rock where feasible.
  • Progressive Rehabilitation: Rehabilitating disturbed areas as soon as they are no longer needed for operations, rather than waiting until final closure.
  • Landform Design for Stability and Function:
    • Engineering waste rock dumps and tailings facilities for long-term geotechnical stability and to control erosion.
    • Shaping landforms to blend with the surrounding landscape, promote drainage, and support the intended post-mining land use.
  • Topsoil Management: Stripping, stockpiling, and respreading topsoil to aid in revegetation. (Chapter 26.4.2).
  • Revegetation: Establishing appropriate plant communities to stabilize landforms, control erosion, and support biodiversity or agricultural use.
  • Relinquishment: Ultimately, the goal is to return the land to a state where it can be relinquished to the government or subsequent land users for a sustainable future.

Key objectives for waste rock storage landform design (Chapter 24.3): 1. Minimize the area occupied (footprint). 2. Ensure the new landform is stable. 3. Ensure the new landform is not more erodible than comparable natural landforms. 4. If ARD is a risk, design to prevent or control its occurrence. 5. Make the surface amenable to rehabilitation for future land use. 6. Ensure the landform is, if possible, congruent with its surrounds (not visually intrusive). Trade-offs are often necessary, e.g., the smallest footprint might involve very steep slopes which are harder to rehabilitate and less stable.

5. Case Study Snippet

Waste Rock Dumps at Batu Hijau, Indonesia

The Batu Hijau copper-gold mine in Sumbawa, Indonesia, involves the movement of vast quantities of waste rock. The design and construction of the waste rock dumps are critical engineering and environmental undertakings. The dumps are designed in terraces to ensure stability and facilitate progressive rehabilitation. Techniques like "bottom-up" construction are used to improve compaction and stability. Careful management of surface water runoff and erosion control are essential due to the tropical high-rainfall environment. (Referenced in Chapter 22, Case 22.3 showing outslope compaction).

6. Conclusion: Shaping a Responsible Mining Legacy

Changes to land use and landform are inherent to most mining operations and can be among the most visible and lasting impacts. Surface mining, and the extensive areas required for overburden, waste rock, and tailings disposal, fundamentally reshape landscapes. However, through careful site selection, integrated mine planning that considers closure from the outset, sound engineering design, progressive rehabilitation, and a commitment to creating stable and productive post-mining land uses, the long-term environmental and social consequences can be responsibly managed. The goal is to minimize the "residual footprint" and ensure that mined lands can support beneficial future uses, whether for ecological conservation, agriculture, or other community needs.
