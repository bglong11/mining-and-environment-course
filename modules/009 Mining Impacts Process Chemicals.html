Handle with Care: Impacts of Process Chemicals in Mining

Focusing on Mercury, Cyanide, and Hazardous Material Management

1. Introduction: Chemicals in Mineral Extraction

Mineral processing often involves the use of chemicals to extract valuable metals from ore. While many of these reagents are relatively harmless or are neutralized during the process, some, like mercury and cyanide, have gained significant public attention due to their toxicity and association with historical environmental incidents. This lesson examines the impacts of these specific chemicals, the broader concerns of hazardous material management, and strategies for safe use, disposal, and impact mitigation.

Lesson Objectives:

  • Understand why mercury and cyanide are used in certain mining processes (primarily gold extraction).
  • Identify the environmental and health risks associated with mercury and cyanide.
  • Recognize that proper management can significantly reduce these risks.
  • Discuss the importance of managing other hazardous and toxic materials used or generated in mining.
  • Outline principles for safe handling, storage, transportation, and disposal of process chemicals and their residues.

2. Cyanide in Gold Extraction: Use and Concerns

Sodium cyanide (NaCN) solutions are widely used in the modern gold mining industry to dissolve gold from finely crushed ore. This process is known as cyanidation or cyanide leaching. (Referenced in Chapter 7.2.2 of "Mining and the Environment").

2.1 Why is Cyanide Used?

  • Effectiveness: Cyanide is highly effective at selectively dissolving gold and silver from ore, even at very low concentrations.
  • Economics: It has historically been a cost-effective method for large-scale gold recovery.

The basic chemical reaction involves gold reacting with cyanide in the presence of oxygen and water to form a soluble gold-cyanide complex.

Cyanidation: A metallurgical technique for extracting gold from low-grade ore by converting the gold to a water-soluble coordination complex. It is the most common leaching process for gold extraction.

2.2 Environmental and Health Risks of Cyanide:

High Toxicity: Cyanide is highly toxic to most living organisms, including humans, fish, and wildlife. It interferes with cellular respiration.

  • Spills and Leaks: Accidental releases of cyanide-containing solutions from tailings dams, pipelines, or processing plants can cause catastrophic fish kills and contaminate water supplies. (Chapter 7.7 & 23).
  • Wildlife Poisoning: Birds and other wildlife can be poisoned if they drink from or come into contact with cyanide solutions in open ponds (e.g., tailings ponds, heap leach ponds).
  • Persistence: While cyanide can degrade naturally under sunlight (photodecomposition) and through biological action, this process can be slow, and cyanide can persist in groundwater or under conditions of low light and oxygen. (Chapter 7.2.4).

2.3 Managing Cyanide Risks:

The mining industry has developed extensive practices to manage cyanide safely, largely codified in the International Cyanide Management Code (ICMC):

  • Containment: Using robust, lined tailings storage facilities and process ponds to prevent seepage and spills.
  • Detoxification: Treating cyanide-containing solutions before discharge or at mine closure to break down cyanide into less harmful substances (e.g., using processes like INCO SO2/Air, hydrogen peroxide). (Chapter 7.2.4).
  • Wildlife Protection: Netting or covering cyanide ponds, using deterrents.
  • Emergency Response Planning: Having detailed plans in place to manage spills or accidental releases.
  • Monitoring: Regular monitoring of water quality, wildlife, and facility integrity.

The ICMC is a voluntary industry program for gold mining companies, and producers and transporters of cyanide, to promote responsible management and reduce environmental risks.

3. Mercury in Mining: A Legacy and Ongoing Challenge

Mercury (Hg) has been historically used in gold mining, particularly in artisanal and small-scale gold mining (ASGM), due to its ability to form an amalgam with gold. (Referenced in Chapter 13.2.2 & 16.3.2 for Artisanal Mining).

3.1 How Mercury is Used in Gold Recovery:

Miners mix liquid elemental mercury with gold-bearing ores or sediments. The mercury binds to the gold particles to form a solid amalgam. This amalgam is then heated, often in open pans or retorts, to vaporize the mercury, leaving behind the gold.

Amalgamation: The process of forming an alloy (amalgam) of mercury with another metal, such as gold. Heating the amalgam separates the mercury by vaporization.

3.2 Environmental and Health Risks of Mercury:

High Toxicity & Persistence: Mercury and its compounds, especially methylmercury, are highly toxic neurotoxins. Elemental mercury vaporized during amalgam heating is a major source of air pollution.

  • Atmospheric Deposition: Airborne mercury can travel long distances before being deposited in soils and water bodies.
  • Methylation: In aquatic environments, inorganic mercury can be converted by bacteria into methylmercury, a highly bioavailable and toxic organic form.
  • Bioaccumulation & Biomagnification: Methylmercury accumulates in aquatic organisms and biomagnifies up the food chain, reaching high concentrations in predatory fish, which can then be consumed by humans and wildlife.
  • Health Impacts: Neurological damage (Minamata disease), developmental problems in children, kidney damage.
  • ASGM Hotspots: Artisanal and small-scale gold mining is the largest anthropogenic source of mercury pollution globally, leading to severe localized and regional contamination.

3.3 Reducing Mercury Use and Impacts:

  • Phasing out Mercury in Large-Scale Mining: Modern large-scale gold mines no longer use mercury due to its environmental risks and the availability of more efficient technologies like cyanidation.
  • Addressing ASGM: This is a complex socio-economic issue. Efforts include:
    • Promoting mercury-free ASGM technologies (e.g., gravity concentration, direct smelting).
    • Training miners on safer practices, including the use of retorts to capture mercury vapor during amalgam heating.
    • Formalizing the ASGM sector to improve regulation and environmental management.
    • Remediation of mercury-contaminated sites.
  • The Minamata Convention on Mercury: An international treaty (entered into force in 2017) designed to protect human health and the environment from anthropogenic emissions and releases of mercury and mercury compounds.

4. Management of Other Hazardous and Toxic Materials

While mercury and cyanide receive significant attention, mines use and generate a range of other potentially hazardous materials that require careful management. (Referenced in Chapter 16.2.4 for "Other Wastes" and 16.8 for "Accidental Environmental Impacts").

  • Acids and Alkalis: Used in various leaching and processing circuits (e.g., sulphuric acid for copper or uranium leaching, caustic soda for alumina). Spills can cause burns and severe environmental damage.
  • Solvents and Reagents: Organic solvents used in solvent extraction (SX) processes, flotation reagents, flocculants. Some can be flammable, toxic, or persistent.
  • Fuels, Oils, and Lubricants: Essential for vehicles and machinery. Spills can contaminate soil and water.
  • Explosives: Residues (e.g., nitrates) can leach into water. Improper storage or handling is a safety risk.
  • Waste Containing Heavy Metals: As discussed with waste rock and tailings, even if not acutely toxic initially, these can become problematic over time through leaching or ARD.

Principles of Hazardous Material Management:

  1. Inventory and Characterization: Knowing what hazardous materials are on site, their quantities, properties, and risks.
  2. Substitution: Where possible, replacing highly hazardous materials with less hazardous alternatives.
  3. Secure Storage: Storing chemicals in designated, bunded (contained) areas, protected from weather, with proper labeling and segregation of incompatible materials.
  4. Safe Handling and Transportation: Training personnel, using appropriate personal protective equipment (PPE), and having clear procedures.
  5. Spill Prevention and Emergency Response: Implementing measures to prevent spills (e.g., secondary containment) and having robust emergency plans to deal with accidents.
  6. Proper Disposal: Disposing of hazardous waste through licensed and approved facilities, according to regulatory requirements. This includes contaminated soils, empty containers, and process residues.

A Safety Data Sheet (SDS), formerly known as Material Safety Data Sheet (MSDS), is a document that provides comprehensive information about a substance or mixture for use in workplace chemical management. SDSs include information on the properties of each chemical; the physical, health, and environmental health hazards; protective measures; and safety precautions for handling, storing, and transporting the chemical. They are a critical resource for risk assessment and emergency response.

6. Conclusion: Responsibility in Chemical Use

The use of process chemicals is often indispensable in modern mining for efficient mineral recovery. However, certain chemicals, notably mercury and cyanide, carry significant environmental and health risks that have rightly attracted public and regulatory scrutiny. While many other concentration chemicals are less intrinsically harmful, all hazardous and toxic materials require meticulous management. This involves a lifecycle approach: from careful selection and substitution where possible, to secure storage, safe handling, robust operational controls, emergency preparedness, and environmentally sound disposal or detoxification of residues. International codes, conventions like Minamata, and best practice guidelines provide frameworks for responsible chemical management, aiming to minimize mining's chemical footprint and protect ecosystems and human health.
